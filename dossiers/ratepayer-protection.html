RATEPAYER PROTECTION FRAMEWORK

POLICY POSITION BRIEF
⚖️ POLICY VERDICT: ADVOCACY FOR LOCAL RATEPAYERS

Fort Worth's economic growth must not come at the expense of residential ratepayers subsidizing regional grid strain. Municipal economic development agreements must include enforceable anti-subsidy, resource-cap, and data-transparency protections. An inquiry is not an adjudication.

1. The Problem: Grid Cost-Shifting

Commercial data center facilities add massive, constant electric loads that drive immediate substation, transmission, and generation capacity upgrades. While wholesale power is regulated at the ERCOT and PUCT levels, the local zoning and economic development incentives that enable these installations are governed by the City of Fort Worth.

Unless municipal development agreements explicitly assign infrastructural and cost-shifting risks to commercial developers, the capital expenditure required to expand local grid infrastructure is recovered by utility providers from the general ratepayer class—resulting in higher monthly utility bills for Fort Worth families.

2. The Three-Point Protection Framework

To insulate residential ratepayers and protect natural resources, Fort Worth municipal policy must adopt the following three-point enforcement framework for all future high-density industrial rezonings:

  1. Anti-Cost-Shifting & Ratepayer Protection Clauses

    Municipal development agreements and tax incentive packages must explicitly evaluate the capital expenditures required for local transmission and substation interconnection. Contracts must contain binding anti-subsidy riders and assign explicit stranded-asset risk to the developer if projected long-term utility loads fail to materialize.

  2. Auditable Consumptive Water Caps

    Industrial water usage for cooling purposes must be bound by contractual maximum caps rather than non-enforceable project estimates. During ERCOT grid alerts or Stage 3/4 municipal drought curtailment phases, data center cooling draws must be subject to mandatory reduction rules with zero contractual exemptions over residential households.

  3. Transparent Public Infrastructure Portals

    Citizens must have open, functional digital access to municipal planning documents, water draw ledgers, and environmental assessments. The city must maintain functional, secure records portals to enable independent citizen audit without vendor-side failures.
